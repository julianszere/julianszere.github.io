Least Springs

Since the elastic potential energy of springs is quadratic, fitting a linear function under least squares is mathematically equivalent to finding the rest state for a linear rod tied to a bunch of springs.

Drag any data point or the rod itself to disturb the equilibrium. The rod always comes to rest on the least squares regression line.

The Least Squares Problem

Given $n$ data points $(x_1, y_1), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)$, ordinary least squares finds the intercept $a$ and slope $b$ that minimise the total squared residual:

$$L(a,\, b) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \bigl(y_i - a - b\, x_i\bigr)^2$$

Setting the partial derivatives to zero and solving gives the familiar normal equations, whose unique solution is the regression line.

The Rod tied to Springs

Now imagine a rigid rod hovering over the scatter plot. The rod has two mechanical degrees of freedom: its vertical position at the centre ($a$, the intercept) and its tilt angle ($\theta \approx b$, the slope for small angles).

At each data point $(x_i, y_i)$ a vertical spring is anchored. The other end attaches to the rod at the same $x$-coordinate, where the rod sits at height $a + b\, x_i$. The spring is stretched (or compressed) by the residual $y_i - a - b\, x_i$, storing elastic energy:

$$U(a,\, b) = \frac{k}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n} \bigl(y_i - a - b\, x_i\bigr)^2$$

With spring constant $k = 2$, this is identical to the least squares objective $L$. The configuration that minimises the total elastic energy is exactly the regression line.

The full Lagrangian for the rod is $\mathcal{L} = T - U$, where $T$ is the kinetic energy, translational plus rotational. Since the equilibrum state of this system will have to extreme $\mathcal{L}$, the rod will settle at the least squares line.
