This project extends the analytical results from the RISK combat models developed by Blatt & Sharon3 and Osborne2 to the Argentine strategy game TEG. Using Markov chains, it computes the probability of conquering a territory after a sequence of attacks.

Although TEG closely resembles RISK, there is one key structural difference: in TEG the defender can roll up to three dice, while in RISK the defender is limited to two. This seemingly minor rule change drastically shifts the balance of the game: as the number of armies grows, the defender's advantage in TEG becomes far greater than in RISK. We'll see why this is the case in detail below.

Single-Battle Probabilities: The Independent CasesProbabilidades de una batalla: los casos independientes

Each round of combat, both attacker and defender roll a number of dice determined by how many armies they have. After rolling, both sides sort their dice in descending order and compare them pair by pair: the attacker's highest vs the defender's highest, second-highest vs second-highest, and so on. The attacker loses one army for each pair in which their die is less than or equal to the defender's die.

The simplest situation, one attacking die vs one defending die, is easy to visualize geometrically.

1 die vs 1 die

Lay out all 36 equally-likely outcomes on a 6×6 grid, with the attacker's result on one axis and the defender's on the other. The attacker wins whenever their die is strictly greater than the defender's (that is, all cells below the main diagonal). The diagonal itself (draws) goes to the defender. Counting:

1 vs 1 case. Each cell is one equally-likely outcome (attacker on x, defender on y). ■ attacker wins (15 cells), ■ defender wins (21 cells).

There are 15 cells below the diagonal (attacker wins) and 21 on or above it (defender wins), giving:

P(attacker wins, 1v1) = 1536 ≈ 0.417    P(defender wins, 1v1) = 2136 ≈ 0.583

These agree exactly with Blatt3 and Osborne.2 No tricks needed here. We simply count cells.

2 dice vs 1 die: a square (the max of two)

When the attacker rolls two dice and the defender one, only the maximum of the attacker's two dice is compared against the defender's single die. COMPLETE

Summing the weighted cells below the diagonal: P(attacker wins, 2v1) = 125/216 ≈ 0.579. The attacker now has the edge.

3 dice vs 1 die: extending to a third dimension

With three attacking dice and one defending die, the attacker's maximum follows an even more skewed distribution: P(max = k) = (3k²−3k+1)/216. Conceptually this is a 6×6×6 cube, collapsed to a 6×6 grid by marginalising over the second and third dice — only the maximum of the three matters here. Summing those cells below the diagonal gives P(attacker wins, 3v1) = 855/1296 ≈ 0.660.

3 vs 1 — attacker axis is the max of three dice (cell width proportional to marginal probability). The attacker wins in 855 of 1296 equally-likely outcomes.

All three cases above involve only a single comparison (the highest die on each side), so the geometric counting is straightforward. Things get harder as soon as we need to compare two pairs simultaneously.

The Independence Trap: N Dice vs 2 or More DiceLa trampa de la independencia: N dados vs 2 o más

When both sides have at least two armies, two pairs of dice are compared: the highest attacker die against the highest defender die, and the second-highest against the second-highest. A natural first instinct is to treat these two comparisons as independent events and multiply their probabilities. This is the approach taken by Tan1 — and it is wrong.

Consider the case of 3 attacking dice vs 2 defending dice (Case VI in the literature). Let Y(1) ≥ Y(2) be the top two attacker dice and Z(1) ≥ Z(2) the top two defender dice. The probability that the defender loses two armies is:

Tan's (incorrect) calculation: P(Y(1) > Z(1)) × P(Y(2) > Z(2)) = 0.471 × 0.551 ≈ 0.259

Correct answer (Osborne): Using the joint distribution of the order statistics, P(Y(1) > Z(1) ∩ Y(2) > Z(2)) = 2890/7776 ≈ 0.372

Why does independence fail? Because Y(1) and Y(2) are the sorted outcomes of the same set of dice. Knowing that the maximum is large makes it more likely that the second-largest is also large — they are positively correlated. Multiplying the marginal probabilities ignores this relationship entirely.

As Osborne2 showed, the correct approach is to work with the joint distribution of (Y(1), Y(2)) and (Z(1), Z(2)) simultaneously. The table below shows how much Tan's values deviate from the correct ones for Case VI (3 attackers, ≥2 defenders):

Outcome Tan (independent) Correct (joint)
Defender loses 2 0.259 0.372
Each loses 1 0.504 0.336
Attacker loses 2 0.237 0.293

The errors are not negligible. Because these transition probabilities are the building blocks of the entire Markov chain, even a small per-roll bias compounds dramatically over the many rounds of a full campaign.

Getting It Right: Joint Distributions and EnumerationEl enfoque correcto: distribuciones conjuntas y enumeración

The correct probabilities for every case are obtained by working with the full joint distribution of the ordered dice. For two dice the joint distribution of (Z(1), Z(2)) is:

P(Z(1)=z1, Z(2)=z2) = 136 if z1 = z2   and   236 if z1 > z2

For three dice the joint distribution of (Y(1), Y(2)) can be derived by marginalising out the minimum:

P(Y(1)=y1, Y(2)=y2) = 3y1−2216 if y1 = y2   and   6y2−3216 if y1 > y2

With these joint distributions in hand, the probability of any outcome is obtained by a double sum (or quadruple sum for the 3v2 case) over all valid combinations of dice values, weighted by the appropriate joint probabilities. Osborne2 and Blatt3 both arrive at the complete set of 14 distinct per-round probabilities this way — exact, not approximate.

The TEG calculator extends this to the additional case where the defender rolls three dice (possible in TEG but not in RISK). The same enumeration technique applies: one works with the joint distribution of (Z(1), Z(2), Z(3)) and sums over all valid comparison outcomes. This is the piece that makes TEG computationally novel relative to prior RISK analyses.

From a Single Round to a Full Battle: Markov ChainsDe una ronda a una batalla completa: cadenas de Markov

A single combat round only removes armies; it never adds them. This means the state of a battle — the pair (a, d) of remaining attacking and defending armies — evolves over time, and the probability of every future outcome depends only on the current state, not on the history of how it was reached. That is exactly the Markov property,1 and it allows us to model an entire battle as a Markov chain.

States and absorbing boundaries

The chain lives on the state space {(a, d) : 1 ≤ a ≤ A, 0 ≤ d ≤ D}. States where d = 0 (attacker wins) or a = 1 (attacker can no longer attack; defender wins) are absorbing — once reached, the battle is over. All other states are transient: the chain will leave them with probability 1 eventually.

A fragment of the state-transition diagram for a small battle. Transient states (white) transition to other transient or to absorbing states (coloured). The per-round probabilities computed in the previous section fill every arrow.

The fundamental matrix

Arranging the per-round probabilities into a transition matrix P in block form:

P = [Q   R ; 0   I]

where Q contains transitions between transient states and R contains transitions from transient to absorbing states.

The probability of eventually reaching each absorbing state from each transient starting state is given by the fundamental matrix F = (I − Q)−1 R.1,2,3 Each row of F corresponds to an initial state (A, D); summing the entries in the columns where the attacker wins yields the victory probability for that starting configuration. Expected losses are computed similarly, by weighting each absorbing state by the number of armies lost and summing over the corresponding row.

For TEG with armies up to 30 on each side, the state space can have close to 1 000 states, making the matrix Q nearly 900×900. Because Q is extremely sparse — each state transitions to at most five others — the inversion is tractable using standard sparse-matrix techniques.

The contrast is stark. In RISK, larger engagements tend to favour the attacker — an intuition consistent with Osborne's2 recommendation to "be more aggressive." In TEG, the opposite holds: the defender's third die provides an advantage that compounds over many rounds, making large-scale attacks a poor proposition unless the attacker has a significant numerical edge.
