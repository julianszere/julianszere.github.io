Orchestrated Pendulums

A set of pendulums — all released from the same angle at the same instant — can be made to sweep through a sequence of coordinated wave patterns and return, after a fixed period, to perfect unison. The trick lies entirely in how their lengths are chosen.

15 pendulums, all released simultaneously. Watch for traveling waves, standing waves, and the eventual return to alignment.

Choosing the lengths

The period of a simple pendulum of length $L$ is $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$, so its angular frequency is $\omega = \sqrt{g/L}$. To produce a repeating collective display, we want each pendulum to complete an integer number of oscillations within a shared recurrence time $\mathcal{T}$.

Label the pendulums $n = 0, 1, \ldots, M-1$. We require pendulum $n$ to complete exactly $N + n$ full oscillations in $\mathcal{T}$:

$$(N + n)\, T_n = \mathcal{T}$$

This fixes each period $T_n = \mathcal{T}/(N+n)$ and hence each length:

$$L_n = \frac{g}{\omega_n^2} = g\left(\frac{\mathcal{T}}{2\pi(N+n)}\right)^2 = L_0\left(\frac{N}{N+n}\right)^2$$

The longest pendulum ($n=0$, slowest) sets the scale $L_0$. Every shorter pendulum is tuned by squaring the frequency ratio, so lengths decrease as $1/(N+n)^2$.

Evenly spaced frequencies

The design condition means angular frequencies are uniformly spaced:

$$\omega_n = \frac{2\pi(N+n)}{\mathcal{T}} = \omega_0 + n\,\Delta\omega, \qquad \Delta\omega = \frac{2\pi}{\mathcal{T}}$$

With all pendulums released from angle $\theta_0$, the position of pendulum $n$ at time $t$ is $\theta_n(t) = \theta_0\cos(\omega_n t)$. The snapshot across all pendulums at a given $t$ looks like:

$$\theta_n(t) = \theta_0\cos\!\bigl(\omega_0 t + n\cdot\Delta\omega\, t\bigr)$$

This is a discrete sinusoid in the index $n$, with apparent wavenumber $\Delta\omega\, t$ that grows linearly in time. As the wavenumber sweeps through half-integer multiples of $\pi$, the ensemble traces out traveling waves, anti-phase clusters, and standing-wave-like patterns before the wavenumber reaches $2\pi$ — at $t = \mathcal{T}$ — and all pendulums snap back into phase simultaneously.

Recurrence and sub-harmonics

Full realignment occurs whenever $t$ is an integer multiple of $\mathcal{T}$, since every pendulum then sits at an integer cycle count. Partial alignments appear at rational fractions of $\mathcal{T}$: at $t = \mathcal{T}/2$ the pendulums split into two groups in antiphase; at $t = \mathcal{T}/3$ and $2\mathcal{T}/3$ three equally spaced clusters form; and so on. The visual rhythm of the display is governed by these simple fractions — a purely arithmetic consequence of the evenly spaced frequencies.
