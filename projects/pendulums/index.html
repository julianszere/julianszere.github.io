Orchestrated Pendulums

Even though these pendulums are completely uncoupled, after realising them from the same angle, they weave through intricate patterns and return to perfect unison after a fixed period of time. This is known as "pendulum waves", but the underlying principle is more general and can be applied to all sorts of periodic trajectories.

Pendulums released simultaneously. Sliders control count, cycles, speed, and angle.

Pendulum Waves

The period of pendulum $n$ is $T_n = 2\pi\sqrt{L_n/g}$. To reproduce the effect of the animation, we want pendulum $n$ to complete exactly $N + n$ full oscillations in a shared recurrence time $\mathcal{T}$. For this to happen,

$$(N+n)\,T_n = \mathcal{T} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad L_n = L_0\!\left(\frac{N}{N+n}\right)^{\!2}$$

There are some great resources online about how this works and its relationship to aliasing. One interesting detail is that this relationship does not enforce anything on the trayectory of each individual pendulum. As long as the frequency of the particles in a system are evenly spaced, this effect will follow.

This leaves space for two interesting generalizations of the phenomenon. First, we don't need to stay in the small angle regime, as the trayectory of the pendulums does not matter, only their frequencies. Second, we can apply the same principle to any periodic system, not just pendulums.

On a line

The simplest case: particles oscillating along the same segment, the $n$-th with amplitude $A$ and angular frequency $\omega_n$:

$$x_n(t) = A\cos(\omega_n t)$$

At $t = 0$ they are all at $+A$. As the frequencies drift apart, the snapshot $n \mapsto x_n(t)$ sweeps through all possible windings of a cosine, producing traveling waves, standing waves, and clusters before collapsing back to $+A$ at $t = \mathcal{T}$.

Each row is one oscillator. Color encodes index $n$.

On a circle

Now let each particle orbit a circle of radius $r_n$ at angular frequency $\omega_n$:

$$(x_n,\, y_n)(t) = r_n\bigl(\cos(\omega_n t),\;\sin(\omega_n t)\bigr)$$

At $t = 0$ every particle is at angle $0$. The phase of particle $n$ is $\phi_n(t) = \omega_0 t + n\,\Delta\omega\,t$, so consecutive particles are always offset by the same $\Delta\phi = \Delta\omega\,t$. As $\Delta\phi$ grows from $0$ to $2\pi$, the ensemble traces one full spiral arm winding. At $t = \mathcal{T}$, $\Delta\phi = 2\pi$ and every particle is back at angle $0$.

Particles on concentric circles. The spiral arm winds and unwinds with period $\mathcal{T}$.

On any curve

More generally, each particle can trace its own closed curve parametrized by a phase $\phi$. Below, particle $n$ traces an ellipse whose x-amplitude grows with $n$ and y-amplitude shrinks.

$$(x_n,\, y_n)(t) = \bigl(a_n\cos(\omega_n t),\;\;b_n\sin(\omega_n t)\bigr)$$

The curves are all different, yet the collective pattern still emerges and still returns to its initial configuration at $t = \mathcal{T}$. The shape of the trajectory is irrelevant; all that matters is that each particle is periodic and the frequencies are evenly spaced.

Particles on ellipses ranging from vertical ($a_n \approx 0$) to horizontal ($b_n \approx 0$).
