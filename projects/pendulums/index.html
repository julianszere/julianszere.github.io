Orchestrated Pendulums

Release a set of pendulums of carefully chosen lengths from the same angle at the same instant. They fan out, weave through intricate wave patterns, and after a fixed period return to perfect unison. The trick is entirely in the lengths.

15 pendulums released simultaneously. Sliders control count, cycles, speed, and angle.

The math

The period of pendulum $n$ is $T_n = 2\pi\sqrt{L_n/g}$, giving angular frequency $\omega_n = \sqrt{g/L_n}$. We want pendulum $n$ to complete exactly $N + n$ full oscillations in a shared recurrence time $\mathcal{T}$:

$$(N+n)\,T_n = \mathcal{T} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad L_n = L_0\!\left(\frac{N}{N+n}\right)^{\!2}$$

The resulting angular frequencies are evenly spaced — an arithmetic sequence with common difference $\Delta\omega = 2\pi/\mathcal{T}$:

$$\omega_n = \omega_0 + n\,\Delta\omega$$

With all pendulums starting from angle $\theta_0$, pendulum $n$ is at $\theta_n(t) = \theta_0\cos(\omega_n t)$. Across the ensemble at any fixed $t$, the phases $\omega_n t$ form an arithmetic sequence in $n$ — a discrete sinusoid in index space whose apparent wavenumber grows linearly. This produces the traveling-wave effect, and at $t = \mathcal{T}$ every pendulum has completed an integer number of cycles and all return to $\theta_0$.

The general principle

Nothing in that argument is specific to pendulums or to oscillation along an arc. The only ingredients are:

1. A collection of uncoupled particles, each following some periodic trajectory.
2. Their frequencies forming an arithmetic sequence.

Given those two conditions the ensemble will dephase, trace complex collective patterns, and realign at $t = \mathcal{T}$ — regardless of the shape of the individual trajectories. The three animations below show the same arithmetic at work on three different curves.

On a line

The simplest case: particles oscillating along the same segment, the $n$-th with amplitude $A$ and angular frequency $\omega_n$:

$$x_n(t) = A\cos(\omega_n t)$$

At $t = 0$ they are all at $+A$. As the frequencies drift apart, the snapshot $n \mapsto x_n(t)$ sweeps through all possible windings of a cosine, producing traveling waves, standing waves, and clusters before collapsing back to $+A$ at $t = \mathcal{T}$.

Each row is one oscillator. Color encodes index $n$.

On a circle

Now let each particle orbit a circle of radius $r_n$ at angular frequency $\omega_n$:

$$(x_n,\, y_n)(t) = r_n\bigl(\cos(\omega_n t),\;\sin(\omega_n t)\bigr)$$

At $t = 0$ every particle is at angle $0$. The phase of particle $n$ is $\phi_n(t) = \omega_0 t + n\,\Delta\omega\,t$, so consecutive particles are always offset by the same $\Delta\phi = \Delta\omega\,t$. As $\Delta\phi$ grows from $0$ to $2\pi$, the ensemble traces one full spiral arm winding. At $t = \mathcal{T}$, $\Delta\phi = 2\pi$ and every particle is back at angle $0$.

100 particles on concentric circles. The spiral arm winds and unwinds with period $\mathcal{T}$.

On any curve

More generally, each particle can trace its own closed curve parametrized by a phase $\phi$. Below, particle $n$ traces an ellipse whose x-amplitude grows with $n$ and y-amplitude shrinks — a family ranging from a thin vertical segment to a wide horizontal one:

$$(x_n,\, y_n)(t) = \bigl(a_n\cos(\omega_n t),\;\;b_n\sin(\omega_n t)\bigr)$$

The curves are all different, yet the collective pattern still emerges and still returns to its initial configuration at $t = \mathcal{T}$. The shape of the trajectory is irrelevant; all that matters is that each particle is periodic and the frequencies are evenly spaced.
