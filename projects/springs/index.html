Least SpringsMínimos Resortes

Since the elastic potential energy of springs is quadratic, fitting a linear function under least squares is mathematically equivalent to finding the rest state of a linear rod tied to a bunch of springs.

Drag any data point or the rod itself to disturb the equilibrium. The rod always comes to rest on the least squares regression line.

Given $n$ data points $(x_1, y_1), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)$, ordinary least squares finds the intercept $a$ and slope $b$ that minimise the total squared residual:

$$L(a,\, b) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \bigl(y_i - a - b\, x_i\bigr)^2$$

Setting the partial derivatives to zero and solving gives the familiar normal equations, whose unique solution is the regression line.

Now imagine a rigid rod hovering over the scatter plot. The rod has two mechanical degrees of freedom: its vertical position at the centre ($a$, the intercept) and its tilt angle ($\tan(\theta) = b$, the slope).

At each data point $(x_i, y_i)$ a vertical spring is anchored. The other end attaches to the rod at the same $x$-coordinate, where the rod sits at height $a + b\, x_i$. The spring is stretched (or compressed) by the residual $y_i - a - b\, x_i$, storing elastic energy:

$$U(a,\, b) = \frac{k}{2}\sum_{i=1}^{n} \bigl(y_i - a - b\, x_i\bigr)^2$$

With spring constant $k = 2$, this is identical to the least squares objective $L$. And since the equilibrium state is achived by minimizing the potential energy, both approaches converge to the same result. Hence, fitting a linear regression is the same as waiting for a linear rod attached to springs to come to its rest state.
